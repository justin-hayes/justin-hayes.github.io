JACT Reading Greek 7F Translation

Sat, Jul 13, 2019

When Euthydemos concludes his argument, Dionysodorus makes the case for the contradiction. Sophists were infamous by Socrates’ day for making both sides of an argument appear equally valid.

Translation

So said Euthydemos. And the students, at the same time both making a din and with laughter, praised this wisdom1. And like a ball Dionysodoros at once received the argument in turn, and on receiving it, he said:

“And why, Kleinias? Let’s suppose the teacher says some arguments. Who learns the arguments, the clever or the ignorant ones?

“The clever,” Kleinias said.

“Then the clever learn, but not the ignorant, and you did not just answer well.2”

And from then making a din and with laughter, the students at once praised this wisdom. And we, falling into perplexity, were silent.

Comments

  1. The independent study guide consistently translates σοφία as cleverness, which better matches the playful tone of these dialogues.
  2. The independent study guide gives the slightly more fluent “…and you did not answer well just now.”
